The book value of an asset such as equipment is an example of:

(A) a future cost

(B) ** a sunk cost.

(C) an opportunity cost.

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -An example of sunk costs in accounting is the book value of existing assets such as fixed assets (e.g., machinery, equipment), inventory, investments, etc. Depreciation, amortization, and impairments also represent sunk costs.

Concept note-2: -The book value of an entity’s fixed assets is a component of an irrelevant cost. It is a sunk cost, which means that the expenditure was incurred previously and will have no impact on future business decisions, regardless of the decision or outcome of its replacement.

Concept note-3: -A sunk cost, sometimes called a retrospective cost, refers to an investment already incurred that can’t be recovered. Examples of sunk costs in business include marketing, research, new software installation or equipment, salaries and benefits, or facilities expenses.

Concept note-4: -A sunk cost refers to money that has already been spent and cannot be recovered. A manufacturing firm, for example, may have a number of sunk costs, such as the cost of machinery, equipment, and the lease expense on the factory.

Concept note-5: -Facilities and Overhead: Similarly, money spent on rent, electric and water bills, and maintenance and other expenses for your physical space are generally sunk costs, as soon as they are paid out.
